This page contains one of the proof of theTychonoff’s Theorem. We want some tools to proove the Tychonoff’s Theorem. We want notation of filters. Further, Munkres proves Tychonoff’s theorem without making mention of filters. Instead, the notion of a maximal collection with the FIP is used.

Definition

Recall that a collection $F$ of subsets of some set $X$ has the finite intersection property (FIP) if for every finite subcollection $\{F_1, F_2, \ldots, F_n\} \subseteq F$ we have \[ \bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i \neq \emptyset \]

Theorem

A topological space $X$ is compact if and only if for every collection $F$ of closed subsets of $X$ with the FIP, we have $\bigcap F \neq \emptyset$.

Proof:

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $X$ is compact and let $F$ be a collection of closed subsets of $X$ with the FIP. We want to show that $\bigcap F \neq \emptyset$. Suppose for contradiction that $\bigcap F = \emptyset$. Then the sets in $F$ cover $X$, and since $X$ is compact, there is a finite subcover, say $\{F_1, F_2, \ldots, F_n\}$. But this contradicts the assumption that $F$ has the FIP, because the intersection of any finite subcollection of $F$ is non-empty.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Now suppose that for every collection $F$ of closed subsets of $X$ with the FIP, we have $\bigcap F \neq \emptyset$. We want to show that $X$ is compact. Let $\{U_i\}$ be an open cover of $X$. We may assume that the $U_i$ are distinct. Define $F = \{X \setminus U_i\}$. Then $F$ is a collection of closed subsets of $X$ with the FIP. By assumption, $\bigcap F \neq \emptyset$, which means that there is a point in $X$ that is not in any $U_i$, contradicting the fact that $\{U_i\}$ is a cover of $X$. Therefore, $X$ must be compact.

∎

Definition

Let $C$ be a collection of subsets of a topological space $X$, with the FIP. We say that $C$ is maximal (with respect to the FIP) if whenever $A$ is a collection of subsets with the FIP such that $C \subseteq A$, then $C = A$.

Proposition

For every collection of closed subsets $C$ with the FIP, there is a maximal $A$ such that $C \subseteq A$.
We will need a theorem (which is called a lemma for historical reasons) from set theory:

Lemma (Zorn's Lemma)

Let $(P, \leq)$ be a (non-empty) partially ordered set such that every linearly ordered subset has an upper bound. Then $P$ contains a maximal element.
The partial order we will consider is the family $T$ of all collections of subsets of a space $X$ with the FIP that contain $C$, ordered by $\subseteq$. Hence, $T \neq \emptyset$ as $C \in T$.

Proof:

Let $C$ be a collection of subsets of $X$ with the FIP. Let $A$ be a totally ordered subset of $T$, the family of all collections of subsets of a space $X$ with the FIP and that contain $C$. We aim to demonstrate that $A$ has an upper bound.

Claim: $\bigcup_{F \in A} F$ is an upper bound for $A$.

It is evident that $\mathcal{F} \subseteq \bigcup_{\mathcal{F} \in A} F$ for each $\mathcal{F} \in A$. The remaining part to prove is that $\bigcup_{\mathcal{F} \in A} \mathcal{F}$ has the FIP. Let $F_1, F_2, \ldots, F_n \in \bigcup_{F \in A} F$, then there exist $\mathcal{F}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{F}_n$ such that $F_i \in \mathcal{F}_i$ for $i=1 \ldots n$. Since $A$ is totally ordered, there exists a $1 \leq N \leq n$ such that $\mathcal{F}_i \subseteq \mathcal{F}_N$ for all $1 \leq i \leq n$. Therefore, $F_i \in \mathcal{F}_N$ for all $1 \leq i \leq n$ and because $\mathcal{F}_N$ has the FIP, $$\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i \in \mathcal{F}_N \subseteq \bigcup_{\mathcal{F} \in A} \mathcal{F}$$. Hence, Zorn s Lemma applies and $T$ has a maximal element as required.

∎

Definition

A collection of subsets $F$ of a space $X$ is called a filter if:
  1. Finite intersections are contained in $F$. That is: $(\forall n \in \mathbb{Z}^{+})(\forall F_1, \ldots, F_n \in F)[\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i \in F]$
  2. $\emptyset \notin F$ and $X \in F$
  3. $F$ is closed upwards. That is if $B \subseteq A \subseteq X$ with $B \in F$ then $A \in F$.
Remark Any filter satisfies the FIP by property (1) and (2).

Definition

Let $F$ be a collection of subsets of a topological space $X$, that is a filter. We say that $F$ is a maximal filter if $\mathcal{A}$ is a filter of $X$ such that $\mathcal{F} \subseteq\mathcal{A}$, then $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{A}$.

Example

For the set of real numbers, denoted by $X = \mathbb{R}$, we can examine the following:
  • $\mathcal{F}_1 := \{(a, b) \subseteq \mathbb{R} : 100 \in (a, b)\}$;
  • $\mathcal{F}_2 := \{A \subseteq \mathbb{R} : \exists (a, b) \ni 100, (a, b) \subseteq A\}$; and
  • $\mathcal{F}_3 := \{A \subseteq \mathbb{R} : 100 \in A\}$.
Here $\mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_2 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_3$ and all three collections have the finite intersection property (FIP). However, $\mathcal{F}_1$ does not qualify as a filter. Because $(99,101)\in \mathcal{F}_1$. But $(99,101)\subsetneq[99,101]\not\in\mathcal{F}_1$. So $\mathcal{F}_1$ does not stratify 3rd condition. While $\mathcal{F}_2$ does, and $\mathcal{F}_3$ is a maximal filter. It is very easy to check other conditions.

Theorem

If $\mathcal{F}$ is a collection of subsets from a set $X$ that is maximal with respect to the Finite Intersection Property (FIP). Then, $\mathcal{F}$ is a maximal filter.

Proof:

We have check 3 conditions of definition of filters. Let's check them one by one.
  1. The first property holds: We need to show that $\mathcal{F}$ have the finite intersections. Let's consider $F_1, ..., F_n\in \mathcal{F}$. The intersection of these elements, $\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i\neq \emptyset$, due to the FIP of \mathcal{F}. It's clear that the set $\{\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i\} \cup \mathcal{F}$ also satisfies the FIP. Therefore, by the maximality of $\mathcal{F}$, we have$\mathcal{F}=\{\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i\} \cup \mathcal{F}$, Thus $\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i\in F$.
  2. The second property holds: Clearly, $\emptyset \notin \mathcal{F}$ as $F$ has the FIP. Thus, $X \cap F = F \neq \emptyset$ for each $F \in \mathcal{F}$. So, $F\cup\{X\}$ has the FIP. By the maximality of $\mathcal{F}$, $\mathcal{F} = F \cup \{X\}$, Thus, $X \in \mathcal{F}$.
  3. The third property holds: This proof is simialr to previous one.

∎

The most crucial part of a filter is property (1). The only other fact about maximal filters we will need is the following way to identify whether a set belongs to the maximal filter.

Theorem

Suppose that $F$ is a maximal filter on a set $X$ and $A \subseteq X$. Then, $$A \in \mathcal{F} \Longleftrightarrow \forall F \in \mathcal{F}, A \cap F \neq \emptyset$$

Proof:

Forward directiona is trivial. Suppose that $A \in F$. By using $F$ has the finite intersection property (FIP) it is very clear that $\forall F \in \mathcal{F}, A \cap F \neq \emptyset$.

Conversely, suppose that $\forall F \in F, A \cap F \neq \emptyset$. We are going to show that $\{A\} \cup \mathcal{F}$ has the FIP, in which case the result follows by maximality of $F$. Let $F_1, \ldots, F_n \in \mathcal{F}$. Since $\mathcal{F}$ is a filter we have that $\emptyset \neq \bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i \in \mathcal{F}$. So by assumption $\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} F_i\right) \cap A \neq \emptyset$. Since $F_1, \ldots, F_n $ are arbitary chosen from $\mathcal{F}$, $\{A\} \cup \mathcal{F}$ has FIP. Since $\mathcal{F}$ is maximal with respect to FIP, then $\mathcal{F}\cup \{A\} =\mathcal{F}$. Therefore, $A\in \mathcal{F}$

∎
